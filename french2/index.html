Le passé composé avec être

Most verbs use avoir as their auxiliary. But 14 special verbs (and their derivatives) use être. With être, the past participle agrees in gender and number with the subject.

🔑 The PAVAN-MRT-PRES-DM mnemonic

🔌 Agreement with être

When you use être as the auxiliary, the past participle agrees with the subject:

SubjectExampleAgreement
il (masc. sg.)Il est parti.—
elle (fem. sg.)Elle est partie.+e
ils (masc. pl.)Ils sont partis.+s
elles (fem. pl.)Elles sont parties.+es

🔄 Derived verbs (also use être)

Verbs derived from the 14 être-verbs also use être:

Base verbDerived verbExample
venirdevenir (to become)Elle est devenue médecin.
venirrevenir (to come back)Ils sont revenus hier.
entrerrentrer (to go back home)Nous sommes rentrés tard.
partirrepartir (to leave again)Elle est repartie ce matin.
Special case: apparaître (to appear) accepts both avoir and être.
Elle est apparue / Elle a apparu — both are grammatically correct.

🤝 Pronominal verbs & agreement

All pronominal verbs (se laver, se regarder, se parler…) use être in the passé composé. But agreement depends on whether the reflexive pronoun is a direct object (COD) or an indirect object (COI).

✓

COD → Agreement

When the reflexive pronoun is a direct object, the past participle agrees.

Nous nous sommes regardés.
(We looked at each other — regarder quelqu'un = COD)

Elles se sont lavées.
(They washed themselves — laver quelqu'un = COD)

✗

COI → No agreement

When the reflexive pronoun is an indirect object, the past participle does not agree.

Nous nous sommes parlé.
(We spoke to each other — parler à quelqu'un = COI)

Elles se sont téléphoné.
(They called each other — téléphoner à quelqu'un = COI)

How to tell the difference? Ask: regarder qui ? (COD) vs. parler à qui ? (COI). If the verb takes à before a person, the reflexive pronoun is a COI → no agreement.

L'impératif — Giving commands

The imperative mood is used for commands, instructions, advice, and suggestions. It exists in only 3 forms: tu, nous, vous — because you can only give commands to people you’re addressing.

💡 Why only 3 forms?

You can only give a command to:

  • tu — one person (informal): Mange !
  • nous — “let’s” (includes yourself): Mangeons !
  • vous — one person (formal) or a group: Mangez !

You cannot command je (yourself alone), il/elle (someone not present), or ils/elles. That’s why the imperative has no subject pronoun and only 3 forms.

📋 What is it used for?

UseExample
Giving an orderArrêtez le café !
Giving adviceNe prends pas de bain trop chaud !
Making a suggestionReposons-nous !
Giving instructionsNe vous levez pas après 10h !
Encouraging someoneSoyez courageux !

⚠️ The -ER verb rule: dropping the -s

For -ER verbs (and aller), the tu form drops the final -s:

VerbPresent (tu)Imperative (tu)
mangertu mangesMange !
parlertu parlesParle !
regardertu regardesRegarde !
allertu vasVa !
But the -s comes back when followed by y or en for pronunciation:
Vas-y ! (Go ahead!) — Parles-en ! (Talk about it!)
Without the -s, “Va-y” or “Parle-en” would sound awkward.

⚡ Exception verbs: être, avoir, savoir

These 3 verbs have irregular imperative forms (they use the subjunctive stem, not the present indicative):

Verbtunousvous
être (to be)soissoyonssoyez
avoir (to have)aieayonsayez
savoir (to know)sachesachonssachez

And for reference, faire (to do/make) is regular in its imperative:

Verbtunousvous
faire (to do)faisfaisonsfaites

Les pronoms relatifs — QUI et QUE

Relative pronouns connect two sentences. The question is: what do we want to focus on? The subject? Use QUI. The direct object? Use QUE.

💡 The core idea: focus

Start with: La femme regarde le film.

What do you want to talk about? The woman (subject)? Or the film (direct object)?

QUI → focusing on the SUBJECT

La femme [qui] regarde le film.
← from: La femme regarde le film.

The word order stays intact: subject + verb + object. QUI simply replaces the subject in the relative clause.

QUE → focusing on the DIRECT OBJECT

Le film [que] la femme regarde.
← from: La femme regarde le film.

The direct object (le film) has been displaced to the front. The subject now comes after QUE.

🇬🇧 The “whom” analogy in English

English “whom” works exactly like QUE: the object moves to the front.

EnglishFrench equivalent
The woman who watches the film. (subject)La femme qui regarde le film.
The film (that/which) the woman watches. (object)Le film que la femme regarde.
The man whom I saw. (object — person)L'homme que j'ai vu.
Key difference: In English, “whom” only applies to humans. In French, QUE applies to all nouns that are direct objects — people and things alike.

🔎 Word order comparison

PronounPatternWord order change?
QUINoun + QUI + verb + restNo — original order intact
QUENoun + QUE + subject + verbYes — object moved to front
Quick test: After the relative pronoun, is the next word a verb? → Use QUI.
After the relative pronoun, is the next word a subject/noun? → Use QUE.

Les pronoms COD — Direct object pronouns

Replace the direct object noun with le, la, l', les to avoid repetition. The pronoun goes before the verb.

📚 The COD pronouns

ReplacesPronounExample
masc. singular nounleMarie regarde ce film. → Elle le regarde.
fem. singular nounlaIls préparent leur exposition. → Ils la préparent.
noun starting with vowell'J'allume mon téléphone. → Je l'allume.
plural noun (any gender)lesIl ne vend pas ses voitures. → Il ne les vend pas.

⚠️ Vowel elision: le/la → l'

When the verb starts with a vowel or silent h, le and la become l':

Full formWith elision
Je le allume ✗Je l'allume. ✓
Il la aime ✗Il l'aime. ✓
Il ne la aime pas ✗Il ne l'aime pas. ✓

📚 COD in the passé composé

In the passé composé, the pronoun goes before the auxiliary (avoir). Watch the vowel elision!

OriginalWith pronoun COD
Nous aimons le film.
Nous avons vu le film trois fois.		 Nous l'aimons.
Nous l'avons vu trois fois.
Nous aimons ces films.
Nous avons vu ces films trois fois.		 Nous les aimons.
Nous les avons vus trois fois.
Agreement rule: In the passé composé with avoir, the past participle agrees with the COD when it comes before the verb:
Nous les avons vus — les (masc. pl.) → add -s.

Le comparatif — Making comparisons

Compare people, things, and quantities using plus, moins, aussi with adjectives/adverbs, and plus de, moins de, autant de with nouns.

📚 Comparing with adjectives & adverbs

ComparisonStructureExample
more (+)plus + adj/adv + queCet appartement est plus spacieux que ton appartement.
less (−)moins + adj/adv + queMa maison a coûté moins cher que ta maison.
as (=)aussi + adj/adv + queMa maison est aussi grande que ta maison.
Adverbs in -ment work the same way:
Elle parle plus rapidement que moi.
Il travaille moins efficacement que son collègue.

📚 Comparing with nouns (quantities)

ComparisonStructureExample
more (+)plus de + noun + queIl y a plus de pièces dans ma maison que dans la tienne.
less (−)moins de + noun + queJ'ai moins d'argent que toi !
as much (=)autant de + noun + queIl y a autant de pièces dans nos deux maisons.

⚠️ The “bon / meilleur” exception

“plus mauvais” exists and is correct. But for bon, you must use meilleur:

✗ Incorrect✓ Correct
*Ce gâteau est plus bon que l'autre.Ce gâteau est meilleur que l'autre.
*Elle est plus bonne en maths.Elle est meilleure en maths.
Meilleur can also mean “best” in certain contexts:
Son meilleur ami = his/her best friend (implies le meilleur ami de X).
Just like in English: “my better half” often means “my best half.”
